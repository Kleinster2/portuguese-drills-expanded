## Outcomes

After this unit, the student can:

- Recognize the most common lexical divergences across daily-life domains (transport, housing, food, technology, body, clothing, family, education)
- Choose appropriate vocabulary for either variant context — order coffee in Lisbon vs São Paulo, ask for a bus in Porto vs Rio, mention furniture in Coimbra vs Salvador
- Avoid the cross-variant vocabulary mishaps that mark a learner as variant-confused

## Grammar

This unit is primarily a vocabulary catalog. There's no grammatical pattern beyond the lexical divergence itself.

### Same-form-different-meaning false friends

A few words exist in both variants with **different meanings** — a higher-stakes type of trap:

| Word | BP meaning | EP meaning |
|---|---|---|
| **propina** | bribe (illegal payment) | tuition (legal university fee) |
| **rapariga** | (regional, sometimes pejorative for a young woman) | girl / young woman (neutral, daily-use) |
| **bicha** | (slur — pejorative) | queue / line (neutral) |
| **camisola** | nightgown | T-shirt / sweatshirt |
| **fato** | event / situation / fact | suit (clothing) |

These false friends across variants are the most embarrassing errors. *Vou pegar a bicha* in EP means "I'm going to get in line"; in BP it's offensive.

### Same-form-same-meaning items

Most vocabulary is shared. The catalog above shows the divergent items; the unmarked majority of words (*água, casa, livro, comer, dormir, falar, gostar*…) work identically in both variants.

### Exemplar mini-paragraphs

Same content, BP vs EP:

**BP:**
> *Hoje de manhã peguei o ônibus para o trabalho. No café da manhã comi pão com presunto e bebi suco de laranja. Almocei na padaria perto do escritório.*

**EP:**
> *Hoje de manhã apanhei o autocarro para o trabalho. No pequeno-almoço comi pão com fiambre e bebi sumo de laranja. Almocei na padaria perto do escritório.*

(Note: *padaria* and *escritório* are shared; the change is mostly in *pegar/apanhar*, *ônibus/autocarro*, *café da manhã/pequeno-almoço*, *presunto/fiambre*, *suco/sumo*.)

## Traps

- **Asking for *trem* in Lisbon yields blank looks.** Use *comboio*. Same in reverse: *comboio* in São Paulo will be parsed as a Portuguese-from-Portugal usage.
- **Saying *casa de banho* in Brazil sounds formal/foreign.** Use *banheiro*. Same in reverse: *banheiro* in EP can be parsed as Brazilian or as referring to a baby's bath area.
- **The false friends are the highest-stakes errors.** *Propina* in EP context (asking about university fees) is fine; in BP context it accuses someone of accepting bribes. *Bicha* in EP is "queue" — neutral; in BP it's a slur. **Always check variant context before using these words.**
- **Some shared words have register asymmetry.** *Vovô / vovó* is universal in BP for grandparents; in EP, *avô / avó* is the standard form (vovô / vovó exists but reads as Brazilian-influenced or affectionate). Default to *avô / avó* when writing for an EP audience.
- **Plural-only clothing items.** *Calças* (trousers) and *cuecas* (underwear) are plural in both variants but BP often uses singular *calça* informally. EP keeps them rigorously plural.
- **The catalog above is starter content, not exhaustive.** Real working vocabulary divergence covers hundreds of items. Use this as a recognition framework; expand domain-by-domain as you encounter real text in either variant.
- **Don't conflate variant divergence with archaism.** Some BP/EP differences reflect older Portuguese forms preserved differently; others are recent neologisms diverging in real time. The catalog above is current standard; older texts may show different distributions.

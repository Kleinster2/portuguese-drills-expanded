## Outcomes

After this unit, the student can:

- Use **que** as the default relative pronoun for both subject and object positions, with both human and non-human antecedents
- Distinguish **restrictive** relative clauses (no commas, defines the antecedent) from **non-restrictive** ones (with commas, adds info about an already-identified antecedent)
- Avoid English-style preposition stranding — Portuguese requires the preposition to precede the relative pronoun

## Vocabulary

- **que** — that / which / who (default relative)
- Common preposition + relative combinations (with *quem* for [+human], *que* otherwise — see `b1-relativo-quem`):
  - *com que / com quem* — with which / with whom
  - *de que / de quem* — of which / of whom
  - *em que* — in which
  - *a que / a quem* — to which / to whom

## Grammar

**Que** is the workhorse relative pronoun: invariable in form, usable for any antecedent (human, non-human, abstract), serving as either subject or direct object of the relative clause.

### Restrictive vs non-restrictive

| Type | Punctuation | Meaning |
|---|---|---|
| Restrictive | no commas | defines / restricts the antecedent (which X?) |
| Non-restrictive | with commas | adds info about an already-known antecedent |

- *O homem **que mora ao lado** é simpático.* — The man who lives next door is nice. (restrictive — *which* man)
- *O João, **que mora ao lado**, é simpático.* — João, who lives next door, is nice. (non-restrictive — adds info about a specific person)

### Subject vs object position in the relative clause

- *A pessoa **que veio** é minha amiga.* — The person who came is my friend. (que = subject of *veio*)
- *O livro **que comprei** está aqui.* — The book that I bought is here. (que = direct object of *comprei*)

Portuguese never drops the relative pronoun. English permits *"the book I bought"* (no relative); Portuguese requires *o livro **que** comprei*.

### Preposition placement

Portuguese **does not allow preposition stranding**. The preposition must come BEFORE the relative pronoun:

| Wrong (English-influenced) | Right (Portuguese) |
|---|---|
| *O homem que falei com.* | *O homem com quem falei.* (use quem after preposition for [+human]) |
| *A casa que moro em.* | *A casa em que moro.* / *A casa onde moro.* |
| *O assunto que falamos sobre.* | *O assunto sobre o qual falamos.* / *O assunto de que falamos.* |

For [+human] antecedents after a preposition, **quem** typically replaces que (see `b1-relativo-quem`). For non-human antecedents, *em que / de que / com que / por que* are common.

### Exemplar sentences

- *Conheço o autor **que escreveu** este livro.* — I know the author who wrote this book.
- *Os filmes **que vi** ontem eram interessantes.* — The films I saw yesterday were interesting.
- *A casa **em que moramos** é antiga.* — The house we live in is old.
- *A Maria, **que é minha vizinha**, vai mudar-se.* — Maria, who is my neighbor, is going to move.
- *Não gosto do café **que serviram**.* — I don't like the coffee they served.
- *O dia **em que nos conhecemos** foi inesquecível.* — The day we met was unforgettable.

## Traps

- **Don't drop the relative pronoun.** English allows *"the book I bought"*; Portuguese requires *o livro que comprei*. The que is mandatory.
- **Don't strand prepositions.** *O homem que falei com* is wrong. The preposition must precede the relative pronoun.
- **Don't overuse o qual.** For most cases, simple *que* is the natural choice. *O qual* / *a qual* (see `b1-relativo-formal-locativo`) is reserved for formal contexts or disambiguation — pulling it out where *que* would suffice sounds bookish.
- **Restrictive vs non-restrictive comma matters semantically.** *Os meus irmãos que moram em Lisboa vêm visitar* (restrictive — only the brothers who live in Lisbon are visiting; implies others don't live there) vs *Os meus irmãos, que moram em Lisboa, vêm visitar* (non-restrictive — all brothers live in Lisbon and they're visiting).
- **Subject que vs object que looks identical.** Disambiguation is positional / semantic. *O homem que viu o João* (the man who saw João — que = subject) vs *O homem que o João viu* (the man whom João saw — que = object). The verb's argument structure tells you which.

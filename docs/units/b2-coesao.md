## Outcomes

After this unit, the student can:

- Use **pronominal reference**, **lexical chains**, and **demonstrative back-reference** to maintain text flow without repetition
- Apply **theme-rheme** progression (given-information first, new-information second) at sentence and paragraph level
- Write paragraphs with strong **topic sentences** and clear thematic progression
- Identify cohesion failures (pronoun ambiguity, paragraph drift, lexical repetition) in their own and others' writing

## Vocabulary

The cohesion toolkit:

### Pronominal reference

- Subject pronouns (often elided): ele, ela, eles, elas
- Object pronouns: o, a, os, as, lhe, lhes
- Reflexive: se
- Demonstrative back-reference: este, esse, aquele (closer ↔ farther in discourse distance)

### Lexical chains

- Synonyms: *casa / habitação / residência / lar*
- Hyponyms / hypernyms: *gato → animal → ser vivo*
- Periphrases: *o autor → o escritor → o nosso interlocutor*

### Discourse markers (transitional)

See [[b1-discourse-markers]] for the foundational set; see [[b2-conectivos-textuais]] for the extended formal-register set.

### Connectives flagging text moves

- **a este respeito** — in this regard
- **além do que foi dito** — beyond what's been said
- **voltando ao ponto** — returning to the point
- **como referido anteriormente** — as mentioned earlier

## Grammar

Cohesion is the **surface-level binding** of a text — the pronouns, connectives, and lexical relations that show which elements are "linked." Coherence is the **deep-level logical sense** — the underlying argument or narrative that makes the text feel like one thing rather than scattered sentences.

Strong texts have both. A text can be cohesive (lots of *este, ele, contudo*) without being coherent (the underlying logic doesn't hold). And a text can be coherent (clear logic) without being well-cohered (sounds like a list of disconnected facts).

### Pronominal reference — antecedent management

The first job of pronouns is to refer back to a previously mentioned entity. The reader must be able to identify the antecedent without ambiguity:

> *(Ambiguous)* O João visitou o Pedro. Ele estava cansado.
>
> Who was tired? Both *João* and *Pedro* are possible antecedents for *ele*. The reader has no way to choose.
>
> *(Disambiguated)* O João visitou o Pedro. **O João** estava cansado da viagem.
>
> Repeating the noun resolves ambiguity at the cost of repetition. Better:
>
> *(Restructured)* Cansado da viagem, o João visitou o Pedro.
>
> The structure puts cause and action in one clause, eliminating the ambiguous pronoun.

When a pronoun has multiple grammatically-possible antecedents and the discourse doesn't disambiguate, restructure the sentence — don't rely on context.

### Subject elision and recovery

Portuguese routinely drops subject pronouns when the verb form makes the subject clear (covered in [[a1-pro-drop]]). In long stretches of text, subject elision builds chains:

> *A Maria chegou cedo. Foi à cozinha. Preparou o café. Bebeu sentada à janela.*

Four sentences, one subject (Maria), explicitly stated only in the first. The verb forms (*chegou, foi, preparou, bebeu*) maintain the subject through morphology. This works when there's no competing subject in the discourse window.

When a competing subject enters, restate explicitly:

> *A Maria chegou cedo. **O João** já estava na cozinha. **Ele** preparou o café e **a Maria** bebeu sentada à janela.*

### Demonstrative back-reference

The three-way demonstrative system (este / esse / aquele) carries discourse-distance information:

- **este / esta / isto** — about to be mentioned, or just mentioned
- **esse / essa / isso** — already mentioned, perhaps not most recently
- **aquele / aquela / aquilo** — mentioned earlier in discourse, more distant

In writing:

- *A questão é a seguinte: **isto** é um problema técnico ou político?* (*isto* points forward to the upcoming clause)
- *Acabei de explicar a regra. **Isso** é o ponto principal.* (*isso* points back to what was just said)
- *Falávamos do projeto X há semanas; **aquele** projeto teve consequências imprevistas.* (*aquele* refers to a more distant discourse referent)

See [[a2-dem-avancados]] for the discourse-and-temporal use of demonstratives.

### Lexical chains — avoiding repetition

A well-cohered text varies its lexical references to the same entity:

> *O autor publicou o livro em 2020. **A obra** tornou-se um best-seller. **O texto** foi traduzido em vinte línguas. **Esta produção** consagrou **o escritor** internacionalmente.*

Five references to the same conceptual entity (the book and its author), each varied: *o livro → a obra → o texto → esta produção → o escritor*. The variation creates lexical density without exhaustion.

The opposite — repetition fatigue — wears the reader out:

> *O autor publicou o livro. O livro tornou-se best-seller. O livro foi traduzido. O autor ficou famoso por o livro.*

### Theme-rheme progression

Sentences typically follow a **given → new** information pattern. The theme (left side) is the known/given information; the rheme (right side) is the new information. Successive sentences chain by promoting the previous rheme to the new theme:

> *(Theme: a economia portuguesa) (Rheme: cresceu 3% em 2024). (Theme: Esse crescimento) (Rheme: foi liderado pelo turismo). (Theme: O setor do turismo) (Rheme: representa hoje 18% do PIB).*

Each sentence's theme picks up the previous sentence's rheme — the text walks forward by handing off topics in a relay.

### Paragraph topic sentences

A well-organized paragraph opens with a **topic sentence** that announces what the paragraph will cover, and closes with either a conclusion or a transition to the next paragraph. Middle sentences develop the topic.

> *O turismo tem sido o motor da recuperação económica nos últimos anos. Os dados mostram um crescimento de 12% no número de visitantes em 2024. Este aumento concentrou-se sobretudo em Lisboa, Porto e Algarve. Contudo, o setor enfrenta agora desafios de sustentabilidade.*

First sentence: announces topic (turismo as economic driver). Middle sentences: develop with evidence and detail. Final sentence: transitions to the next theme (sustainability challenges).

## Traps

- **Pronoun ambiguity is the most common cohesion failure.** When two grammatically-possible antecedents exist, the reader stalls. Restructure rather than relying on context.
- **Paragraph drift.** A paragraph that opens with one topic and ends with another loses readers. Each paragraph should hold one main idea; transitions to new ideas mark new paragraphs.
- **Lexical repetition fatigue.** Repeating *o livro* five times in a paragraph wears down the reader. Vary with synonyms (*a obra, o texto*), pronouns (*ele*), or demonstratives (*esta produção*).
- **Demonstrative confusion.** *Isto* (forward-pointing or just-mentioned), *isso* (mentioned earlier), *aquilo* (more distant) carry discourse-distance information. Substituting one for another shifts meaning. *Falei sobre isto* (this thing I'm about to discuss) vs *Falei sobre isso* (that thing I just discussed) vs *Falei sobre aquilo* (that distant matter from earlier).
- **Theme-rheme breaks.** When successive sentences fail to chain (each starts with brand-new information), the reader can't follow the thread. Promote the previous rheme to the next theme — or signal the topic shift explicitly.
- **Cohesion vs coherence — both required.** A text full of *contudo* and *além disso* but with weak underlying argument feels mechanically constructed. Conversely, a coherent argument with no transitional markers feels jumpy. Both layers need attention.

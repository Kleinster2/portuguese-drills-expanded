## Outcomes

After this unit, the student can:

- Use **cujo / cuja / cujos / cujas** as the possessive relative pronoun ("whose")
- Apply the agreement rule that's counterintuitive for English speakers: **cujo agrees with the THING POSSESSED, not with the possessor**
- Avoid the common error of inserting an article between cujo and the noun (*cujo o nome* — wrong; *cujo nome* — right)
- Recognize cujo as semi-formal — common in writing, less common in casual speech

## Vocabulary

- **cujo** (m sg) — whose [m sg thing]
- **cuja** (f sg) — whose [f sg thing]
- **cujos** (m pl) — whose [m pl thing]
- **cujas** (f pl) — whose [f pl thing]

## Grammar

Cujo is the possessive relative pronoun. It links an antecedent (the possessor) to a possessed noun in the relative clause. The agreement is the high-leverage rule:

**Cujo agrees with the noun that follows it (the possessed thing), NOT with the antecedent (the possessor).**

This inverts English speakers' intuition, where "whose" is invariable and the agreement question doesn't arise.

### The agreement rule

- *A mulher **cujo nome** esqueci.* — The woman whose name I forgot.
  - *cujo* is masculine singular because **nome** (the possessed thing) is masc sg, NOT because *mulher* is feminine.
- *O homem **cuja casa** visitamos.* — The man whose house we visited.
  - *cuja* is feminine singular because **casa** is fem sg, NOT because *homem* is masc.
- *A escola **cujos alunos** foram premiados.* — The school whose students were awarded.
  - *cujos* is masculine plural because **alunos** is masc pl.
- *O livro **cujas páginas** estão rasgadas.* — The book whose pages are torn.
  - *cujas* is feminine plural because **páginas** is fem pl.

### No article between cujo and the noun

This is mandatory and a common error point:

| Wrong | Right |
|---|---|
| *A mulher cujo o nome esqueci.* | A mulher **cujo nome** esqueci. |
| *O homem cuja a casa visitamos.* | O homem **cuja casa** visitamos. |

Cujo absorbs the article function — adding *o/a* on top is ungrammatical.

### Cujo can follow a preposition

- *O autor **a cujo livro** me referi…* — The author whose book I referred to…
- *A empresa **em cujos escritórios** trabalho…* — The company in whose offices I work…

When a preposition combines with the article-bearing word in the noun phrase, contractions apply: *de* + *cujo* → *de cujo* (no contraction needed since cujo isn't an article); *em* + *cujos* → *em cujos*. The contractions students know from *de + o = do* don't apply to cujo itself.

### Exemplar sentences

- *Conheci um escritor **cuja obra** vou ler.* — I met a writer whose work I'll read.
- *A casa **cujo telhado** está partido precisa de obras.* — The house whose roof is broken needs repairs.
- *Contratámos a empresa **cujas referências** eram melhores.* — We hired the company whose references were better.
- *O cliente **cujo pedido** chegou primeiro será atendido primeiro.* — The customer whose order arrived first will be served first.
- *Estudei com professores **cujos métodos** ainda uso.* — I studied with professors whose methods I still use.

## Traps

- **The agreement-with-possessed rule is the biggest trap.** English speakers default to agreeing cujo with the antecedent (*a mulher cuja nome* — wrong, because *nome* is masc not fem). Drill the rule explicitly: look at the noun AFTER cujo, not the noun before.
- **Don't insert an article.** *Cujo o nome* is wrong; *cujo nome* is right. Cujo replaces the article.
- **Register: semi-formal.** Cujo is standard in writing and educated speech. In very casual conversation, speakers sometimes work around it: *A mulher que esqueci o nome dela* (with anaphoric *dela*) — substandard but heard. Teach cujo as the target form; don't endorse the workaround.
- **Cujo doesn't combine with quem.** *A pessoa cujo livro li* is right (the person whose book I read). *A pessoa de quem o livro li* is awkward — when possession is the relation, use cujo.
- **Don't confuse possession with restrictive description.** *A pessoa cujo carro avariou* (the person whose car broke down) vs *A pessoa que tem o carro vermelho* (the person who has the red car). Cujo specifically marks possession-of-X-by-Y; *que tem* is just description.

## Outcomes

After this unit, the student can:

- Map any 2nd-person address form (*tu, você, o senhor, a senhora*) to its register and variant
- Read or write in either variant with appropriate pronoun choice for the social context
- Recognize the **register inversion** of *você* — informal/default in BP, formal/distant in EP — and avoid the resulting cross-variant errors

## Vocabulary

The 2nd-person address landscape in modern Portuguese:

| Pronoun | BP register | EP register |
|---|---|---|
| **tu** | rare (regional, far South / Northeast) | informal default |
| **você** | informal default | formal / distant |
| **o senhor / a senhora** | very formal | very formal |
| **vocês** | 2nd-person plural (all registers) | 2nd-person plural (all registers) |
| **vós** | extinct in modern speech | extinct in modern speech |

## Grammar

### The você inversion

This is the single highest-leverage BP/EP difference for any learner switching tracks:

- **In BP**: *você* is the **default informal** form. You use *você* with friends, family, peers, children, strangers your own age. *O senhor / a senhora* is reserved for formal/respectful contexts (older strangers, professional registers, very formal letters).
- **In EP**: *você* is **formal/distant**. With friends and family you use *tu*. *Você* enters when there's professional distance, age difference without intimacy, or a deliberate signal of respect-and-distance. *O senhor / a senhora* is super-formal.

Direct cross-variant transfer fails:
- A BP speaker using *você* with new EP friends sounds **cold/distant**, not friendly.
- An EP speaker hearing BP *você* may parse it as inappropriately formal — the BP intent of casual friendliness misreads.

### Conjugation correlate

Each pronoun selects a verb-form pattern:

| Pronoun | Verb form | Example |
|---|---|---|
| tu | 2nd-person singular (-s ending) | *Tu **falas** português?* |
| você (BP or EP) | 3rd-person singular | *Você **fala** português?* |
| o senhor / a senhora | 3rd-person singular | *O senhor **fala** português?* |

For a student writing in EP, the practical rule: switching from *tu* to *você* keeps the verb form same as switching to *o senhor*. The verb cues address-distance via grammatical 3rd person, while *tu* keeps you in 2nd person.

### Imperatives

Imperative forms diverge by pronoun choice:

| Address | Affirmative imperative | Negative imperative |
|---|---|---|
| tu (EP) | *Fala!* (bare 3sg pres ind) | *Não fales!* (subjuntivo presente 2sg) |
| você (BP and EP) | *Fale!* (subjuntivo presente 3sg) | *Não fale!* (subjuntivo presente 3sg) |

A BP speaker sees only the *você*-imperative form day-to-day; an EP speaker uses both depending on who's being addressed. See `a2-imperativo-bp` and `a2-imperativo-ep` for full paradigms.

### Vós is dead — historical note

Both modern variants have lost productive use of *vós* (the 2nd-person plural informal). Some grammar tables still list *vós* paradigms (*vós sois, vós tendes*) but no native speaker uses these in everyday speech. The 2nd-person plural is now *vocês* in both variants, regardless of register.

Encounters with *vós* in modern texts are typically:
- Liturgical / religious language (especially in Catholic prayers)
- Law / formal proclamations
- Literary or poetic register
- Quotations from older texts

### Exemplar contrasts

- BP: *Você quer um café?* (asking a friend) → EP equivalent: *Queres um café?* (tu, no pronoun shown — natural in EP)
- BP: *O senhor pode me ajudar?* (asking an older stranger) → EP equivalent: *(O senhor) pode ajudar-me?* / *Pode ajudar-me?* (similar register, similar form)
- BP: *Vocês vão à festa?* (asking a group) → EP equivalent: *Vocês vão à festa?* (identical — *vocês* shared)

## Traps

- **The você inversion is the #1 cross-variant error.** A BP-trained student moving to EP must consciously substitute *tu* for *você* in friend/family contexts. An EP-trained student moving to BP must accept that *você* is now warm and unmarked.
- **Don't over-correct toward *tu* in BP.** While some BP regions (Rio Grande do Sul, parts of the Northeast) use *tu* productively, the standard urban BP has lost it. A BP speaker hearing *tu* outside those regions parses it as either regional, archaic, or affected. Don't import EP *tu* habits into BP production.
- **The *o senhor / a senhora* form requires actually saying it.** Unlike *tu* / *você*, which can be dropped (verb agreement carries the reference), *o senhor / a senhora* is typically explicit — dropping it loses the register marker. *O senhor pode me ajudar?* (right) vs *Pode me ajudar?* (drops the senhor — register weakens).
- **Vós is a recognition target.** Recognize it in liturgy, law, literature. Don't produce it in conversation — it sounds either reverent (in religious contexts) or absurd (in casual ones).
- **Plural vocês is a register-flattener.** With a group of friends in EP, *vocês* is the natural form (no plural-tu equivalent in modern use). The intimacy that *tu* expresses is lost in plural — both variants are equally flat at the 2nd-plural level.

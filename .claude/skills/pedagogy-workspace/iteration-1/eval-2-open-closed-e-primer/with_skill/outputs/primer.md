# Open É vs Closed Ê — Brazilian Portuguese (paulista)

## The two sounds

Stressed E in Portuguese has two qualities. They are different sounds, and swapping one for the other can change the word.

- **Closed Ê** — sounds like the *ai* in English **bait**.
- **Open É** — sounds like the *e* in English **bed**.

Lead with the closed sound. It's the one your English ear already hears in *você* and *português*, and it appears in the highest-frequency words.

## Hear the contrast (real-accent minimal pair)

When the accent is written, it tells you the sound:

- *você* (you) = vo-**SEH** — closed
- *é* (is) = **EH** — open
- *café* (coffee) = ka-**FEH** — open
- *português* (Portuguese) = poor-too-**GEHS** — closed

The circumflex (ê) marks closed. The acute (é) marks open. When you see one of these accents, the sound is decided for you.

## When no accent is written

Most stressed Es in Portuguese carry no accent at all. The sound still has to be either closed or open — but the spelling won't tell you which. You learn it word by word.

- *mesa* (table) = **MEH**-zah — closed
- *festa* (party) = **FEHS**-tah — open

Same plain *e* on the page, different sounds in the mouth. There's no rule to memorize here. When you meet a new word, listen for the quality and lock it in.

## What the accents are not

The acute and circumflex appear only when (a) the stress falls somewhere unusual, or (b) the word needs to be distinguished from another. They are not a general open/closed marker that gets sprinkled on every E. Don't expect every open E to wear an acute — most don't.

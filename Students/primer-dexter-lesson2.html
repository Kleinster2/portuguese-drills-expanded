Pronúncia — Open and Closed O

In Lesson 1 you learned open é vs closed ê. The same distinction exists for O.
Open ó = like “aw” in “law”: avó (grandmother), móro (I live), móra (you live)
Closed ô = like “oh” in “go”: avô (grandfather), ônibus (bus)

Classic pair: avó (grandmother) vs avô (grandfather) — one sound changes the meaning.

In -ar verbs like morar: the stressed O is always open —
• eu móro • você móra • same open ó in both forms
• Same for: gósto / gósta, tóco / tóca

Revisão — What You Can Say (Lesson 1)

In Lesson 1 you learned to make statements about yourself:

Eu sou a Dexter. Sou americana. Sou do Brooklyn.
Eu moro em Nova York. Eu falo inglês e espanhol.

And to redirect with: E você?

Now you’ll learn to ask real questions — so you can drive the conversation.

Perguntas Sim/Não — Yes/No Questions

Good news: In Portuguese, yes/no questions have the same word order as statements. Only your voice rises at the end.

Statement ↓Question ↑
Você mora em Nova York.Você mora em Nova York?
Você fala português.Você fala português?
Você é do Brasil.Você é do Brasil?
Recall from Lesson 1: Answer by echoing the verb —
• Sim, moro. / Não, não moro.
• Sim, falo. / Não, não falo.

Onde / De onde — Where / From Where

onde = where • de onde = from where

Teacher Notes — Lesson 2

Pronunciation Introduced

  • Open ó vs closed ô: avó (grandmother) vs avô (grandfather)
  • In -ar verbs, the stressed O is always open: móro, móra (no alternation)
  • Note: -er/-ir verbs DO alternate (e.g., cômo vs cóme) — that comes in a later lesson

What Lesson 2 Gives the Student

She can now ask (você):

  • Where someone lives — Onde você mora?
  • Where someone is from — De onde você é?
  • Where someone works — Onde você trabalha?
  • What someone studies/speaks — O que você estuda?
  • Yes/no questions — Você fala português?

She can produce the você forms:

  • é, mora, trabalha, estuda, fala

New vocabulary:

  • onde = where • de onde = from where • o que = what
  • agora = now • também = also • Que legal! = Cool!

Grammatical tools added:

  • Question formation with question words (onde, de onde, o que)
  • Yes/no question intonation
  • Você conjugation pattern for -ar verbs (-a ending)

What she still cannot do:

  • Ask “how” questions (no como yet — waits for estar)
  • Talk about anyone else (no ele/ela)
  • Express age, likes, wants, or needs
  • Use any verb beyond the original five
  • Handle anything past or future
  • Express possession (no ter)

The leap: She goes from reactive (answering, redirecting) to active (asking questions, driving the conversation). The dialogue is the first real two-way exchange.

Parte I — Pronúncia

Closed vs Open E

• ê (closed) — like “ai” in “bait”: você (you), português
• é (open) — like “e” in “bed”: é (is), até (until)
Back-to-back: você é (you are) — closed ê, then open é.

Closed vs Open O

• ô (closed) — like “oh” in “go”: sou (I am), como (how)
• ó (open) — like “aw” in “law”: ótimo (great), moro (I live), gosto (I like)
Classic pair: avô (grandfather) vs avó (grandmother) — one sound changes the meaning.

You can’t always tell from the spelling. A stressed O with no accent could be either sound:
• como (how) = KÔH-moo — stressed O is closed
• moro (I live) = MÓH-roo — stressed O is open
Both words are written without any accent. Same spelling style, different sounds — you learn which goes with which word.

Unstressed vowel reductions

Final -o and -os = “oo”: trabalho (I work) = trah-BAH-lyoo • Estados Unidos (United States) = is-TAH-doos oo-NEE-doos

Initial unstressed es- = “is”: Estados = is-TAH-doos, estou = is-TÔH, escola (school) = is-KÓH-lah — Brazilians reduce the initial e to an i sound

Diphthongs

• eu = ÊH.oo: eu (I) — closed ê + a light “oo” off-glide
• ou = ÔH (single sound in BP): sou (I am) = SÔH, estou (I am — temporarily) = is-TÔH — the “ou” collapses to a single closed ô in BP

Nasal vowels (sound through the nose)

• ão: não (no), São Paulo — like “ow” but nasal
• -im: sim (yes) — nasal “een”
• em / êm: em (in), bem (well), também (also) — nasal “eyn”

Special consonants

• LH = “ly”: trabalha (you work) = trah-BAH-lyah
• Final L = W: Brasil = brah-ZEW • legal (cool) = lêh-GAW

estou and está

estou / está (to be — temporarily)
• eu estou (I am) = is-TÔH — closed ô (like sou)
• você está (you are) = is-TAH — open stressed a
bem (well/fine) = bêhn — nasal “eyn” (same sound as in também)

Parte II — Falando de Você (Talking About Yourself)

Identity — Eu sou

ser = to be (permanent) • eu sou = I am • você é = you are
Portuguese uses articles before names: Eu sou a Maria. Eu sou o Pedro. (I am Maria. I am Pedro.)

Nationalities change for gender: -o (male) / -a (female)

Origin — Eu sou de / do / da / dos

de = of, from. Contracts only when the next word has a definite article.
de + articleResultExampleMeaning
de + odoEu sou do BrasilI am from Brazil
de + adaEu sou da ColômbiaI am from Colombia
de + osdosEu sou dos Estados UnidosI am from the United States
No article → no contraction: cities (Eu sou de São Paulo — I am from São Paulo), fields (professor de português — teacher of Portuguese).

Residence — Eu moro em / no / na / nos

morar = to live • eu moro = I live • você mora = you live
em = in, on, at — same contraction pattern as de:
• em + o = no: Eu moro no Brasil (I live in Brazil)
• em + a = na: Eu moro na Colômbia (I live in Colombia)
• em + os = nos: Eu moro nos Estados Unidos (I live in the United States)
Cities stay bare: Eu moro em Nova York. (I live in New York.)

Yes/No questions — same word order, rising voice

In Portuguese, yes/no questions use the same word order as statements. Only your voice rises at the end.

Answer by echoing the verb (conjugated for eu):
• Você mora no Brooklyn? (Do you live in Brooklyn?) → Sim, moro. (Yes, I do.) / Não, não moro. (No, I don't.)
• Você fala português? (Do you speak Portuguese?) → Sim, falo. (Yes, I do.) / Não, não falo. (No, I don't.)
• Você é do Brasil? (Are you from Brazil?) → Sim, sou. (Yes, I am.) / Não, não sou. (No, I'm not.)

Question words — onde, de onde, o que

• onde = where • de onde = from where • o que = what
ÔHN-jee • jee ÔHN-jee • oo kee

Parte IV — Como Você Está? (How Are You?)

estar — The other “to be”

Portuguese has two verbs for “to be”:
• ser — permanent, defining: who you are, where you’re from.
• estar — temporary, changeable: how you feel, where you are right now.

 serestar
eusouestou
vocêéestá
is-TÔH • is-TAH

Same sentence, different verb, different meaning:
• Eu sou americano. = I am American. (permanent)
• Eu estou cansado. = I am tired. (right now)

como — How

como = how; as, like (KÔH-moo)
Same word order as the other question words: como + você + verb.

Question (PT)Answer (PT)English
Como você está?Eu estou bem.How are you? — I'm well.
Como você está?Estou cansado.How are you? — I'm tired.
In Portuguese, the subject is often dropped when the verb makes it clear:
• Estou bem. = I am well. (no need for eu)
• Está cansado? = Are you tired? (no need for você)

The greeting exchange

The most common opening in Brazil goes like this:

— Oi! Tudo bem?Hi! All good?
— Tudo ótimo! E você?All great! And you?
— Estou bem, obrigado.I’m fine, thanks.
Answers on a scale:
ótimo (great) > bem (fine) > mais ou menos (so-so) > mal (bad)

Closing a conversation: Até logo = See you later

Teacher Notes

Pronunciation

  • Final -o / -os → “oo” / “oos”
  • Nasal vowels: ão, -im, em / êm
  • LH = “ly”; final L = W
  • Closed ê vs open é
  • Closed ô vs open ó
  • Diphthongs eu, ou
  • estou (closed ô + ou) / está (open stressed á) contrast

Capabilities this primer builds

Can do:

  • Complete self-introduction (name, nationality, origin, residence, work/study, languages)
  • Redirect with E você?
  • Answer yes/no questions with the verb echo
  • Ask yes/no questions via intonation
  • Ask and answer onde / de onde / o que / como questions
  • Greeting exchange (Tudo bem? — Tudo ótimo, e você?) and close with Até logo
  • Express how the student feels right now (bem, mal, cansado/a, ocupado/a, com fome/sede/frio/calor)
  • Distinguish ser (permanent) from estar (temporary) in simple sentences

Grammar tools:

  • Six verbs: ser, morar, trabalhar, estudar, falar, estar (eu + você forms)
  • Contractions de/do/da/dos, em/no/na/nos + when NOT to contract
  • Articles before names (o/a)
  • Question formation: yes/no (intonation) and four question words (onde, de onde, o que, como)
  • Subject-drop: estou / está can stand alone without eu / você
  • Idiomatic com for physical states (com fome, com sede, etc.)

Out of scope (not addressed here):

  • Age, likes, wants, needs (no ter, gostar, querer, precisar)
  • Talking about others by name (no ele/ela/eles/elas conjugations)
  • Plural subjects (nós, vocês, eles)
  • Past or future tenses
  • Verbs beyond the six

Pedagogical notes

Spanish-speaking students: estar, como, and the ser/estar split will feel familiar. The main surprises are (a) the com fome idiom (Spanish uses tengo hambre, Portuguese uses estou com fome), and (b) the open/closed O distinction (Spanish doesn’t mark it).

Subject-drop: This primer encourages dropping the subject (Estou bem instead of Eu estou bem). Brazilians often keep eu, but Estou alone is natural — worth flagging so the student doesn’t feel they’re breaking a rule.

Phase discipline: This primer stays 100% in eu and você per PEDAGOGY Principle 10. No ele/ela is used, even though está doubles as the 3rd-person form.

O Problema do “Duplo Sentido”
In Brazilian Portuguese, the imperfeito (imperfect) form of certain verbs carries two completely different meanings depending on context:

Meaning 1 — Past Habitual: “used to / was” → describes a past state or habit.
Meaning 2 — Polite/Conditional: “would / could / should” → softens a request, gives advice, or expresses a wish right now.

The form is identical. Only context tells you which meaning is intended.

VerboFormaPassado Habitual (P)Polidez / Condicional (C)
quererqueria“used to want”“would like”
poderpodia“used to be able to”“could (you)?”
deverdevia“owed / used to owe”“should” (advice)
tertinha“used to have / had”“would you have?”

Context Clues:
• Past markers → Past Habitual: quando era criança, antes, naquela época, antigamente, todo domingo
• Present anchoring → Polite/Conditional: por favor, para mim?, hoje, agora, com licença
Por que alguns verbos NÃO têm esse problema?

Not all verbs suffer from this ambiguity equally. The gradient exists BECAUSE of disambiguation:

• querer, poder, dever, ter — HIGH ambiguity. Present-moment context usually makes the meaning clear, so speakers freely use the imperfeito for both meanings.
• gostar, saber, preferir — LOW ambiguity. The imperfeito form (gostava, sabia, preferia) sounds like past habitual almost exclusively. To avoid confusion, speakers keep the condicional (gostaria, saberia, preferiria) for polite/conditional meaning.

Key insight: The reason “gostaria” exists in polite speech (instead of “gostava”) is precisely because “gostava” would be misread as “used to like.” The system self-corrects to stay clear!

D. Por Que Alguns Verbos Evitam o Problema?

Para cada situação abaixo, escolha a forma que um brasileiro usaria naturalmente: imperfeito ou condicional. Depois, explique por quê em inglês (1–2 frases). Pense na ambiguidade!
Exemplo: Você está no Toby’s Estate Café em Manhattan e quer pedir um café. Você diz:
“Eu _______ um flat white.” (querer)
Resposta: queria (imperfeito) — Because “queria” in a café ordering context is clearly polite/present. No one would think you “used to want” a flat white. The present-moment anchoring removes all ambiguity.

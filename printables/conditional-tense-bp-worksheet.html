Futuro do Pretérito — O Condicional em Português Brasileiro

Português Brasileiro • Aluno: Student • Data: _______________
Formação — Verbos Regulares:
O futuro do pretérito é formado com o infinitivo completo + terminações. As terminações são as mesmas para todos os verbos (-ar, -er, -ir):
SujeitoTerminaçãofalarcomerabrir
eu-iafalariacomeriaabriria
você / ele / ela-iafalariacomeriaabriria
nós-íamosfalaríamoscomeríamosabriríamos
a gente-iafalariacomeriaabriria
vocês / eles / elas-iamfalariamcomeriamabririam
Formação — Verbos Irregulares (apenas 4!):
Estes verbos usam um radical especial encurtado + as mesmas terminações (-ia, -ia, -íamos, -iam):
InfinitivoRadicaleuvocênóseles
fazerfar-fariafariafaríamosfariam
dizerdir-diriadiriadiríamosdiriam
trazertrar-trariatrariatraríamostrariam
pôrpor-poriaporiaporíamosporiam
Atenção: O radical de pôr é por- (sem acento!).
Quando usar o Futuro do Pretérito:

1. Hipotéticos (would): Expressa o que aconteceria em uma situação imaginária.
• Com mais dinheiro, eu compraria uma casa maior. (With more money, I would buy a bigger house.)

2. Cláusulas com “se” (if-clauses): A cláusula com se usa o imperfeito do subjuntivo; a cláusula principal usa o condicional.
• Se eu pudesse, viajaria o mundo inteiro. (If I could, I would travel the whole world.)

3. Futuro do passado (reported speech): Relata o que alguém disse/pensou/prometeu que faria.
• Ele disse que viria à festa. (He said he would come to the party.)
• Ela prometeu que traria os documentos. (She promised she would bring the documents.)

💡 Dica: No português brasileiro falado, o imperfeito às vezes substitui o condicional para pedidos educados — mas isso é tema de outra lição!

Teacher Notes — Futuro do Pretérito (Nível 1)

Student Profile

This worksheet is designed as a general-purpose Level 1 drill for the Brazilian Portuguese conditional tense (futuro do pretérito). It is suitable for intermediate learners who have already been introduced to the present and past tenses and are now encountering the conditional for the first time. No prior knowledge of the subjunctive is required beyond recognizing se-clause patterns provided in the reference box.

Scope & Boundaries

This worksheet covers ONLY three things:
1. Conjugation — forming the conditional for regular verbs and the four irregulars (fazer, dizer, trazer, pôr).
2. Se-clause hypotheticals — filling in the conditional in the main clause when the se-clause uses the imperfeito do subjuntivo.
3. Future of the past (reported speech) — using the conditional to report what someone said/thought/promised would happen.

NOT covered: the imperfeito/condicional gradient for polite speech, softened requests, or double-duty disambiguation. These are separate drills.

Key Pedagogical Points

  • Formation is highly regular: Emphasize that the conditional uses the full infinitive as the stem (unlike the present tense). Students who remember this find the tense very approachable. The endings (-ia, -ia, -íamos, -iam) are identical across all three conjugation groups.
  • Only four irregulars: fazer → far-, dizer → dir-, trazer → trar-, pôr → por-. The stems are shortened, but the endings remain the same. Help students notice the pattern: the “-zer” ending drops to create the short stem.
  • Accent on nós forms: The accent on -íamos is critical. Students frequently omit it. Stress that this accent indicates the syllable stress falls on the “i” (faríamos, not *fariamos).
  • “A gente” takes 3rd person singular: This is a common stumbling block. Reinforce that although a gente means “we,” it conjugates like ele/ela.
  • Indefinite pronouns: Todo mundo, alguém, and ninguém all take 3rd person singular. Exercise 10 (todo mundo) and 18 (ninguém) test this.

Spanish-Speaking Learners

For students with Spanish knowledge, the conditional is a major area of positive transfer. The formation is nearly identical (infinitive + endings). The four irregular stems have close Spanish parallels:
• PT far- ↔ ES har- (hacer)
• PT dir- ↔ ES dir- (decir) — identical!
• PT trar- ↔ ES traer- (traer) — note Spanish adds -dr- (traería)
• PT por- ↔ ES pondr- (poner) — Spanish adds -dr-
Highlight that Portuguese drops more from the stem than Spanish does.

Section-by-Section Teaching Tips

Section A (Regular Verbs): Start here. Have students identify the infinitive, then simply add the ending. If students struggle, have them write out the full paradigm for one verb before continuing.

Section B (Irregular Verbs): Before starting, have students memorize the four short stems. A mnemonic: “Far, Dir, Trar, Por — Four Different Tricky Parts.”

Section C (Se-clauses): Students do NOT need to produce the subjunctive — it is provided. Their only task is the conditional in the main clause. If students ask about the subjunctive forms, acknowledge them briefly but keep focus on the conditional.

Section D (Reported Speech): Emphasize the “future of the past” concept: at a past moment, someone looked forward to something that hadn’t happened yet. Only the conditional is correct here — the imperfeito would change the meaning to habitual past.

Section E (Translation): Accept reasonable variations. The answer key provides model answers, but word order may vary. Key criterion: correct conditional form.

Section F (Free Production): Use these for formative assessment. Look for correct conditional conjugation, proper subject-verb agreement, and appropriate hypothetical context.

Common Errors to Watch For

  • Using the present tense instead of the conditional (“*eu compro” instead of “eu compraria”)
  • Missing accent on -íamos forms
  • Using the full infinitive stem for irregulars (“*fazeria” instead of “faria”)
  • Keeping the circumflex on pôr (“*pôria” instead of “poria”)
  • Confusing a gente conjugation (“*a gente faríamos” instead of “a gente faria”)

Follow-Up Drills

After this worksheet, students should progress to:

  • Conditional: Usage — the imperfeito/condicional gradient for polite speech and softened requests
  • Conditional: Disambiguation — distinguishing conditional from imperfect in ambiguous contexts

O Gradiente Imperfeito / Condicional na Fala Educada (BP)

No português brasileiro falado, o imperfeito frequentemente substitui o condicional em pedidos educados, conselhos, desejos e opiniões suavizadas. Mas isso não é uniforme — depende do verbo e da construção.
VerboImperfeitoCondicionalTendênciaNota

deverdeviadeveria~80% imp.“Deveria” soa mais formal.
tertinhateria~60% imp.Ambas são naturais.
poderpodiapoderia~50/50Igualmente naturais.
sabersabiasaberia~60% cond.“Saberia” é mais comum em pedidos.
preferirpreferiapreferiria~60% cond.Ambas usadas; condicional levemente preferido.

💡 Regras Dependentes da Construção

Quanto mais a frase puder ser lida como passado habitual, mais os falantes preferem o condicional para evitar ambiguidade:

• Pedidos diretos ancorados no presente → imperfeito funciona bem:
   “Eu queria um café.” (claramente um pedido, não passado)

• Perguntas abertas → condicional é mais seguro:
   “O que você faria?” (não “fazia” — que soa como “costumava fazer”)

• Terceira pessoa sem âncora no presente → condicional é mais seguro:
   “Ele preferiria ficar.” (não “preferia” — que soa como hábito passado)

Alternativa para “querer”: Em vez de “quereria” (forma morta), use “gostaria de” para formalizar.

Teacher Notes — Imperfeito vs Condicional: Fala Educada

Student Profile

This worksheet is designed for an intermediate-level student of Brazilian Portuguese who has already mastered conditional tense conjugation (Level 1) and is now ready to tackle the pragmatic question of when Brazilians actually use the imperfeito vs the condicional in polite, softened speech. No prior knowledge of this gradient is assumed.

Key Insight: This is a usage/pragmatics drill, not a conjugation drill. The student already knows the forms — the challenge is knowing which form is natural in which context. This distinction is rarely taught explicitly but is critical for sounding natural in spoken BP.

Pedagogical Goals

  1. Internalize the verb-by-verb gradient — from querer (~100% imperfeito) to gostar (~95% condicional).
  2. Understand construction-dependent rules — direct present-moment requests vs. open questions vs. third-person statements.
  3. Recognize “quereria” as a dead form and use “gostaria de” as the formal alternative.
  4. Develop register awareness — knowing how to shift between informal (imperfeito) and formal (condicional) registers.

Section-by-Section Notes

Section A (Fill-in-the-blank): The most structured section. Students choose the most natural form. Key teaching moments: #1 (queria, not quereria), #3 (gostaria, not gostava), #7 (both forms acceptable for preferir). If a student consistently uses the condicional for all verbs, redirect them to the gradient table.

Section B (Register rewriting): Tests whether the student can move between registers. The critical items are #12 and #14, where formalizing “queria” requires switching to “gostaria de” rather than using the nonexistent “quereria.”

Common Error: Students may try to formalize “Eu queria...” by writing “Eu quereria...” This is the single most important correction in this drill. “Quereria” is grammatically valid but completely dead in BP. The formal equivalent is “Eu gostaria de...”

Section C (Multiple choice with justification): Forces metalinguistic awareness. Students must not only choose but explain why. Key items: #17 (open questions favor condicional), #20 (third person without present anchoring favors condicional), #22 (“quereria” is dead).

Section D (Contextual fill-in): Similar to Section A but with more varied subjects (ninguém in #25). Watch for #25 — even with “ninguém” as subject, “queria” is natural because querer is ~100% imperfeito.

Section E (Translation): The most productive closed-form section. Students must produce full sentences. Watch for natural BP word order and proclisis (e.g., “me dizer” not “dizer-me”).

Section F (Free response and reflection): #39 and #40 test explicit metalinguistic knowledge. Accept answers in English or Portuguese. The key concepts: “quereria” is dead; “gostava” fails as polite because it’s too easily read as past habitual for a stative verb.

Differentiation Tips

  • Struggling students: Have them complete only Sections A and C first, using the grammar box as a direct reference. Then move to B and D.
  • Advanced students: Challenge them to complete Section F with more elaborate answers, or ask them to create their own sentence pairs showing the imperfeito/condicional contrast for each verb on the gradient.
  • Spanish-speaking students: Note that Spanish ALWAYS uses the condicional for polite speech (querría, debería, podría). The BP imperfeito-for-politeness pattern has no equivalent in Spanish. However, for gostar/saber/preferir, BP behaves similarly to Spanish (condicional preferred). This can be a useful anchor.
What Comes Next: After this drill, the student should move to Level 3: Conditional — Ambiguity, which explores WHY some verbs can use the imperfeito freely (because context disambiguates) while others keep the condicional (because the imperfeito would be misread as past habitual). That drill builds directly on the gradient introduced here.

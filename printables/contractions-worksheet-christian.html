Contrações — Preposição + Artigo

Português Brasileiro • Student: Christian • Date: _______________

How contractions work: In Portuguese, when a preposition (de, em, por, a) sits next to a definite article (o, a, os, as), they always merge into one word. You can't say "de o" — it must be "do." This worksheet practices all four contraction families.

de + artigo definido

de = from, of   |   Used for: origin, possession, description

o (masc. sg.) a (fem. sg.) os (masc. pl.) as (fem. pl.)
de do da dos das

Part A: de + artigo (1–8)

Fill in the blank with do, da, dos, or das.

1. Eu gosto café com leite. (I like coffee with milk.)
2. A família minha esposa é de São Paulo. (My wife's family is from São Paulo.)
3. As chaves carro estão na mesa. (The car keys are on the table.)
4. Eu sou Brooklyn. (I'm from Brooklyn.)
5. O endereço restaurante está no celular. (The restaurant's address is on my phone.)
6. Nós gostamos praias do Rio. (We like the beaches of Rio.)
7. A cor paredes é azul. (The color of the walls is blue.)
8. Ele é amigo meus vizinhos. (He's a friend of my neighbors.)

Países com e sem artigo

Most countries take a definite article. When a preposition meets that article, contract as usual.

WITH article: o Brasil, a Argentina, o México, a Colômbia, os Estados Unidos, o Canadá, a França, a Alemanha, a Espanha, a Itália, a Inglaterra, o Japão

WITHOUT article: Portugal, Cuba, Moçambique, Angola

Examples: de + o Brasil = do Brasil  |  em + a França = na França  |  de + Portugal = de Portugal (no article to contract)

Common error: "Eu vou pra Brasil" — missing the article! It's para o Brasil (or pro Brasil in casual speech). The article is always required for countries that have one.

mais vs mas

mais = more (quantity, comparison) — "Eu quero mais café." / "Ela é mais alta."

mas = but (contrast) — "Eu gosto, mas é caro."

Trick: if you can replace it with "however", it's mas. If you can replace it with "additional", it's mais.

Teacher Notes — For Gil Only

Why this worksheet, why now

Christian finished pretérito perfeito in February and has had a month off. Rather than push into imperfeito, this session firms up a foundation gap that will contaminate everything he produces going forward: preposition + article contractions. His WhatsApp messages show consistent errors here ("por as aulas", "vou pra Brasil", "ate na 10 minutos"), and these are structural — they show up in every sentence, not just in specific tense constructions.

Language interference analysis

Christian's language background (Haitian Creole, French, English, some Spanish) is actively working against him on contractions. Here's why, family by family:

1. The "de" contractions (do, da, dos, das) — partial help from French

French contracts de + le = du and de + les = des. So the idea that "de" merges with a masculine article isn't foreign to him. But French does not contract de + la — it stays "de la" (de la France, de la maison). Portuguese contracts all four: do, da, dos, das. Prediction: he'll do better with do (parallel to French du) but may resist da because French "de la" stays split.

2. The "em" contractions (no, na, nos, nas) — no help from any language

This is the biggest gap. None of his languages contract a locative preposition with an article:

  • FR "dans le", "dans la", "en" — no contraction
  • HT "nan" — no articles to contract with
  • ES "en el", "en la" — no contraction
  • PT "em + o = no" — mandatory contraction, unique to Portuguese

He has zero L1/L2 intuition for these. Every "no/na/nos/nas" has to be built from scratch. His error "ate na 10 minutos" (should be "até em 10 minutos") actually shows over-application — he's starting to learn that em contracts, but applied it where there's no article (just a number). That's a good sign: the rule is entering his system, just imprecisely.

3. The "por" contractions (pelo, pela, pelos, pelas) — no help from any language

Same story as "em." French "par le/la/les" never contracts. Spanish "por el/la/los/las" never contracts. His error "obrigado por as aulas" is a direct Spanish/French transfer — he's writing the preposition and article as separate words, exactly as they'd appear in every other language he knows.

This is probably his weakest contraction family because: (a) "por" contractions are less frequent than "de" or "em," so he's had less exposure; (b) the resulting forms (pelo, pela) are morphologically opaque — they don't look like "por + o" the way "do" looks like "de + o."

4. The "a" contractions (ao, à, aos, às) — partial help from French

French à + le = au and à + les = aux map fairly well onto Portuguese ao and aos. But French doesn't contract à + la (it stays "à la"), while Portuguese gives us à (with crase). The crase concept itself — two identical vowels merging with an accent — has no parallel in French, English, or Creole.

5. Countries and articles — French gives false confidence

French and Portuguese largely agree on which countries take articles (le Brésil / o Brasil, le Japon / o Japão). So his instinct about article assignment should be OK. The problem is what happens next:

  • FR Feminine countries use "en" with no visible article: "en France", "en Italie"
  • PT All countries with articles contract normally: "na França", "na Itália"

French "en France" (preposition alone, article absorbed) may be reinforcing his "em Brasil" / "pra Brasil" pattern — the article feels unnecessary because it disappears in French.

6. mais vs mas — a direct false cognate from French

This one is straightforward and worth pointing out to him explicitly:

Language "but" "more"
FR French mais plus
PT Portuguese mas mais
ES Spanish pero más

French mais = "but." Portuguese mais = "more." The spelling is identical, the meaning is swapped. When Christian writes "mais" meaning "but," he's producing the French word with Portuguese spelling. The fix is a one-time "aha" moment — once he sees the table, the confusion should resolve quickly. Spanish más (more) reinforces the Portuguese meaning, which helps.

Teacher Notes — Session Plan

Warm-up (10 min)

  • Ask "O que você fez no último mês?" — review pretérito perfeito, see how much stuck after the break.
  • Listen for contraction errors in his answers — they'll come up naturally when he talks about places and people.
  • Don't correct yet — just note what he gets right and wrong to calibrate the session.

Preterite worksheet review (15 min)

  • Go through the homework (preterite worksheet 2) he received Feb 18.
  • Focus on what he struggled with, not full review. Skip what he got right.
  • If he didn't do it, don't dwell — move to contractions.

Contractions teaching (40 min)

  • Sections A-B first. "de" and "em" are the highest frequency. If he can nail these, that's already a big win.
  • Show him the French parallel explicitly for "de" contractions: "You already do this in French — du pain, not de le pain. Portuguese just does it with ALL the articles, not just masculine." This leverages what he knows.
  • For "em" contractions, flag the novelty: "French doesn't do this. Spanish doesn't do this. This is new."
  • Section C (por) — exercise 17 is his actual error. Use it as the anchor: "Remember when you wrote 'obrigado por as aulas'? Here's why." Make it concrete and personal.
  • Section F (mais/mas) — show him the French/Portuguese table. This should click instantly.

Mixed practice (15 min)

  • Do Section G together in class. These are the hardest because he has to identify the preposition AND contract it.
  • Exercise 50 is the capstone: all three skills (das, mas, mais) in one sentence.

Homework

  • Any sections you didn't cover in class become homework.
  • Ask him to correct his own WhatsApp messages from the past month — find 5 messages where he should have used a contraction. Real-world application.

What to watch for

  • Over-correction: after learning contractions, he might start contracting where he shouldn't (e.g., "em 10 minutos" becoming "no 10 minutos" — numbers don't take articles).
  • "da" resistance: if he gets "do" right but hesitates on "da/das", that's the French "de la" interference.
  • "pelo/pela" avoidance: he might restructure sentences to avoid "por + article" entirely. If you notice him dodging "por," push him into it.

Next session

If contractions are solid after this worksheet, the natural next step is imperfeito (imperfect tense). His French imparfait gives him the concept for free — the payoff will be the pretérito vs imperfeito contrast, which is where conversation becomes natural.

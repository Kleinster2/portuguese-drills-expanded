Genérico ou Específico? — “de” vs “do/da”

Português Brasileiro • Student: _______________ • Date: _______________

When do you contract? When do you leave “de” alone?

You already know that de + o = do, de + a = da, etc. But that only happens when the noun has a definite article. If the noun is generic — no article — the preposition stays as plain de.

Generic (de) Specific (do/da/dos/das)
Eu gosto de café.
I like coffee.		 Eu gosto do café que ela faz.
I like the coffee she makes.
Ela tem medo de cachorro.
She’s afraid of dogs.		 Ela tem medo do cachorro do vizinho.
She’s afraid of the neighbor’s dog.
A casa é de madeira.
The house is made of wood.		 A cor da madeira é bonita.
The color of the wood is beautiful.
Quick test: Can you point to THE specific one? → do/da. Are you talking about the thing in general? → de.

Teacher Notes

Purpose

This worksheet is the companion to the contractions worksheet (100 exercises). That worksheet drills how to contract. This one drills when — the student must decide whether the noun takes a definite article or not.

Use this AFTER contractions are automatic. If the student is still hesitating on do/da/dos/das, it’s too early for this worksheet — introducing “sometimes you don’t contract” will undermine the muscle memory he’s building.

What forces the article (and thus the contraction)

  • Relative clauses: “o café que ela faz” → do
  • Possessives: “a ajuda do professor” → da
  • Superlatives: “o melhor do mundo” → do
  • Nouns that inherently require the article: “o escuro” → do

What blocks the article

  • Generic/mass nouns: café, música, chocolate, futebol → de
  • Material: de madeira, de vidro, de couro → de
  • Abstract topics: de política, de trabalho → de
  • Most cities: de São Paulo, de Nova York → de

Watch out: adjectives don’t reliably force the article

“Eu gosto de café brasileiro” and “Eu gosto do café brasileiro” are both valid. The adjective nudges toward the article but doesn’t force it. That’s why this worksheet avoids adjective-only contexts — every “specific” exercise uses a structural marker (relative clause, possessive, or superlative) that unambiguously requires the article.

Tricky exercises

  • Exercise 26 (“do escuro”): “O escuro” always takes the article in BP. The student might try “de escuro” by analogy with the generic pattern. Use this to teach that some nouns are inherently definite.
  • Exercise 33 (two blanks, both “de”): The student who’s been training contractions may try “do vinho” or “dos vinhos.” Both are generic here.
  • Exercise 36 (capstone): Three blanks — de, do, do. Same noun (“café”) appears twice with different answers. This is the “aha” moment: the word doesn’t change, the context does.

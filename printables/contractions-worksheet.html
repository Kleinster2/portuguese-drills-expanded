How contractions work: In Portuguese, when a preposition (de, em, por, a) sits next to a definite article (o, a, os, as), they always merge into one word. You can't say "de o" — it must be "do." This worksheet practices all four contraction families.

Países com e sem artigo

Most countries take a definite article. When a preposition meets that article, contract as usual.

WITH article: o Brasil, a Argentina, o México, a Colômbia, os Estados Unidos, o Canadá, a França, a Alemanha, a Espanha, a Itália, a Inglaterra, o Japão

WITHOUT article: Portugal, Cuba, Moçambique, Angola

Examples: de + o Brasil = do Brasil  |  em + a França = na França  |  de + Portugal = de Portugal (no article to contract)

Common error: "Eu vou pra Brasil" — missing the article! It's para o Brasil (or pro Brasil in casual speech). The article is always required for countries that have one.

mais vs mas

mais = more (quantity, comparison) — "Eu quero mais café." / "Ela é mais alta."

mas = but (contrast) — "Eu gosto, mas é caro."

Trick: if you can replace it with "however", it's mas. If you can replace it with "additional", it's mais.

Teacher Notes

Why this worksheet, why now

Preposition + article contractions are a foundation gap that will contaminate everything a student produces if not addressed. Common errors include "por as aulas", "vou pra Brasil", "ate na 10 minutos" — these are structural and show up in every sentence, not just in specific tense constructions.

Language interference analysis

A student with French, Haitian Creole, English, and/or Spanish background will find that their L1/L2 is actively working against them on contractions. Here's why, family by family:

1. The "de" contractions (do, da, dos, das) — partial help from French

French contracts de + le = du and de + les = des. So the idea that "de" merges with a masculine article isn't foreign. But French does not contract de + la — it stays "de la" (de la France, de la maison). Portuguese contracts all four: do, da, dos, das. Prediction: the student will do better with do (parallel to French du) but may resist da because French "de la" stays split.

2. The "em" contractions (no, na, nos, nas) — no help from any language

This is the biggest gap. None of his languages contract a locative preposition with an article:

  • FR "dans le", "dans la", "en" — no contraction
  • HT "nan" — no articles to contract with
  • ES "en el", "en la" — no contraction
  • PT "em + o = no" — mandatory contraction, unique to Portuguese

A French/Spanish speaker has zero L1/L2 intuition for these. Every "no/na/nos/nas" has to be built from scratch. A common error is "ate na 10 minutos" (should be "até em 10 minutos") — this actually shows over-application, meaning the rule is entering the system, just imprecisely. That's a good sign.

3. The "por" contractions (pelo, pela, pelos, pelas) — no help from any language

Same story as "em." French "par le/la/les" never contracts. Spanish "por el/la/los/las" never contracts. A common error is "obrigado por as aulas" — a direct Spanish/French transfer, writing the preposition and article as separate words, exactly as they'd appear in those languages.

This is probably the weakest contraction family because: (a) "por" contractions are less frequent than "de" or "em," so there's less exposure; (b) the resulting forms (pelo, pela) are morphologically opaque — they don't look like "por + o" the way "do" looks like "de + o."

4. The "a" contractions (ao, à, aos, às) — partial help from French

French à + le = au and à + les = aux map fairly well onto Portuguese ao and aos. But French doesn't contract à + la (it stays "à la"), while Portuguese gives us à (with crase). The crase concept itself — two identical vowels merging with an accent — has no parallel in French, English, or Creole.

5. Countries and articles — French gives false confidence

French and Portuguese largely agree on which countries take articles (le Brésil / o Brasil, le Japon / o Japão). So the student's instinct about article assignment should be OK. The problem is what happens next:

  • FR Feminine countries use "en" with no visible article: "en France", "en Italie"
  • PT All countries with articles contract normally: "na França", "na Itália"

French "en France" (preposition alone, article absorbed) may reinforce an "em Brasil" / "pra Brasil" pattern — the article feels unnecessary because it disappears in French.

6. mais vs mas — a direct false cognate from French

This one is straightforward and worth pointing out explicitly:

Language "but" "more"
FR French mais plus
PT Portuguese mas mais
ES Spanish pero más

French mais = "but." Portuguese mais = "more." The spelling is identical, the meaning is swapped. When a French speaker writes "mais" meaning "but," they're producing the French word with Portuguese spelling. The fix is a one-time "aha" moment — once they see the table, the confusion should resolve quickly. Spanish más (more) reinforces the Portuguese meaning, which helps.

What to watch for

  • Over-correction: after learning contractions, students may start contracting where they shouldn't (e.g., "em 10 minutos" becoming "no 10 minutos" — numbers don't take articles).
  • "da" resistance (French speakers): if the student gets "do" right but hesitates on "da/das", that's "de la" interference — French doesn't contract the feminine.
  • "pelo/pela" avoidance: students may restructure sentences to avoid "por + article" entirely. If you notice them dodging "por," push them into it.

Suggested sequence

  • Sections A-B first. "de" and "em" are the highest frequency. If the student nails these, that's already a big win.
  • Section G last. Mixed practice is the hardest — the student has to identify the preposition AND contract it. Exercise 100 is the capstone: five blanks in one sentence.
  • Follow-up: the companion worksheet Genérico ou Específico? teaches when NOT to contract. Use only after contractions are automatic.
